# Nuclear energy is the best path to decarbonization (Baseline)

## Round 1

### Defender

Nuclear power offers a unique combination of advantages for decarbonization. It generates massive amounts of electricity with virtually zero greenhouse gas emissions during operation. A single nuclear plant can produce around 8 TWh per year — you'd need thousands of wind turbines or millions of solar panels to match that output.

The intermittency problem with renewables is well-known. The sun doesn't always shine and the wind doesn't always blow. This means we need backup power, which today comes mainly from natural gas. Nuclear doesn't have this problem — it runs 24/7 at over 90% capacity factor. Countries like France that invested heavily in nuclear have among the cleanest electricity grids in the world, while Germany, which shut down its nuclear plants, has had to rely more on coal and gas.

Nuclear also has the smallest land footprint of any major energy source. A nuclear plant uses about 1 square mile to generate a gigawatt of power. Solar farms need 75 times more land, and wind farms need even more. In a world where land use and biodiversity are already under pressure, this matters.

The waste issue is often overstated. All the nuclear waste ever produced in the US fits in an area the size of a football field stacked 30 feet high. Compare that to the millions of tonnes of CO2 emitted daily by fossil fuels. Modern reactor designs can even use spent fuel as feedstock, dramatically reducing waste volume.

### Skeptic

The Defender paints an optimistic picture but ignores some fundamental challenges. The biggest one is cost. New nuclear plants are staggeringly expensive. Vogtle Units 3 and 4 in Georgia cost $35 billion — more than double the original estimate — and took 7 years longer than planned. That's not an outlier. Nearly every Western nuclear project in the last two decades has suffered massive cost overruns.

Meanwhile, solar and wind costs have plummeted. Solar is now the cheapest form of electricity generation in history, according to the IEA. You can build a solar farm in 1-2 years for a fraction of the cost of a nuclear plant that takes 10-15 years to build. In a climate emergency, speed matters enormously. Every year we spend building a nuclear plant is a year we could have been generating clean power from renewables.

The Defender mentions France, but even France is struggling. Its fleet of aging reactors has been plagued by maintenance issues — in 2022, nearly half were offline for corrosion repairs, forcing France to import electricity from Germany. The Flamanville EPR, France's new-generation reactor, is 12 years late and three times over budget.

On waste: it's not about volume, it's about hazard. No country on Earth has opened a permanent repository for high-level nuclear waste. The US spent decades and billions on Yucca Mountain before abandoning it. Finland's Onkalo repository is the closest to operational. Saying the waste "fits on a football field" trivializes a problem that remains unsolved after 70 years of commercial nuclear power.

## Round 2

### Defender

The Skeptic focuses on cost, and I acknowledge that recent Western projects have had serious cost overruns. But this is not inherent to nuclear technology — it's a consequence of decades of policy neglect. South Korea and China build reactors on time and on budget at a fraction of Western costs. The UAE's Barakah plant was built by Korean contractors for about $5,500 per kilowatt, compared to Vogtle's $16,000.

The real question is total system cost, not just the cost of individual plants. When you add up the cost of solar panels, wind turbines, battery storage, grid upgrades, and backup generation needed for a renewables-only system, the numbers change dramatically. MIT's Future of Energy Storage study found that costs escalate sharply once you pass 80% renewable penetration. California, one of the most solar-heavy grids in the world, regularly curtails solar production while still relying on natural gas for 40% of its electricity.

Battery storage is improving, but we're nowhere close to solving the multi-day or seasonal storage problem. Current battery capacity globally is about 45 GWh. A renewables-only US grid would need thousands of times that. Hydrogen is proposed as a long-duration solution, but it loses 60-70% of the input energy in conversion.

As for France's 2022 problems — that was a maintenance backlog, now largely resolved. France still has electricity emissions of about 50g CO2/kWh, compared to Germany's 350g despite Germany's massive renewable investment. The facts speak clearly about which approach delivers lower emissions.

### Skeptic

The Defender raises valid points about system costs, and I'll concede that comparing bare LCOE numbers without accounting for integration costs is misleading. But the response to this isn't necessarily nuclear — it could also be better grid design, demand flexibility, and geographic diversity.

The Korea and China comparison is appealing but misleading for most of the world. These programs succeeded under conditions that don't exist in democratic, market-based economies: state-owned utilities, centralized decision-making, limited public opposition, and continuous building programs. The US hasn't built a new reactor (before Vogtle) in 30 years. The specialized workforce has largely retired. Rebuilding that capability takes decades, and we need decarbonization now.

On batteries: the technology is advancing rapidly. CATL and BYD are commercializing sodium-ion batteries that don't use lithium or cobalt. Costs have dropped 90% in the last decade. Iron-air batteries from Form Energy promise 100-hour storage at $20/kWh. I agree we're not there yet, but betting on continued improvement in a technology that's been consistently beating projections seems safer than betting on nuclear, which has been consistently missing them.

The mineral supply issue cuts both ways, by the way. Nuclear requires enriched uranium, and fuel supply chains are concentrated in Russia and Kazakhstan. The geopolitics of nuclear fuel are at least as fraught as those of battery minerals — arguably more so given the weaponization potential.

## Round 3

### Defender

I want to address the "we need it now" argument directly. There are 440 nuclear reactors operating worldwide right now, generating about 10% of global electricity with near-zero emissions. The single most impactful thing we can do in the short term is keep them running. Every nuclear plant that closes gets replaced partially or fully by fossil fuels — we saw this in Germany, Japan, and several US states.

For new build, I accept that Western democracies face real challenges. But some countries are proving it can be done in democratic contexts — South Korea is a democracy, as are France and the UK, all of which are building or planning new reactors. The key is political commitment to a long-term program, not authoritarian governance.

Small modular reactors represent a potential game-changer for costs and timelines, though I acknowledge this is still largely aspirational. NuScale's first project was cancelled due to cost increases, which is a setback.

My core argument is this: no credible pathway to net-zero excludes nuclear. The IEA, IPCC, and virtually every major energy scenario includes nuclear expansion. It's not either/or — we need nuclear AND renewables. But nuclear addresses the hardest part of the problem (reliable, baseload, zero-carbon power) that nothing else can currently fill at scale.

### Skeptic

I largely agree that existing nuclear plants should be kept running — that's a cost-effective way to maintain clean baseload. Where I disagree is on the proposition that nuclear is "the best path."

Let's look at the numbers. Global nuclear capacity is about 413 GW. Even optimistic scenarios add maybe 200-300 GW by 2050. That's perhaps an additional 2,000-2,500 TWh per year. We need to add roughly 15,000-20,000 TWh of clean generation by 2050. Nuclear, at best, covers 10-15% of the new capacity needed. The rest has to come from somewhere — and that somewhere is overwhelmingly renewables.

So nuclear can be an important part of the solution, especially for firm power. But calling it "the best path" implies it should be the primary strategy. The math doesn't support that. The best path is rapid renewable deployment supplemented by storage, grid improvements, demand flexibility — and yes, nuclear where it makes sense.

I think where we've landed is reasonable: nuclear is a valuable component, not the best single path. The debate framing created a false choice. The reality is a portfolio approach, where each technology does what it does best.

## Verdict

**Strongest arguments:**

Defender: The system cost argument — that renewable LCOE without integration costs is misleading — is well-supported. France vs. Germany emissions comparison is compelling. The existing fleet preservation argument is pragmatically strong and hard to counter.

Skeptic: The cost and timeline evidence for new nuclear construction in Western democracies is devastating. The capacity math (nuclear can supply 10-15% of needed new generation) directly challenges the "best path" framing.

**Key disagreements:**
- Whether nuclear's construction and cost challenges are fixable policy problems or structural limitations
- Whether battery storage improvements will solve the multi-day problem in time
- Whether "best path" requires volume dominance or just addressing the hardest sub-problem

**Assessment:** Both sides made reasonable arguments. The Skeptic has a stronger case against the specific proposition ("best path") because nuclear simply cannot provide the volume needed. The Defender has a stronger case for nuclear's unique value in providing firm, baseload clean power. The debate converged on a portfolio approach. Confidence in the proposition: 5/10.
