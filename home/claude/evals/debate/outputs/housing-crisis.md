# Debate: The housing crisis is primarily a supply problem

## Round 1

### Defender

The material conditions of housing in the United States are as follows. The National Association of Realtors estimates a housing deficit of 5.5-6.8 million units as of 2023. The median existing home price reached $389,800 in 2023 (NAR), while median household income was approximately $80,610 (Census Bureau), yielding a price-to-income ratio of ~4.8, well above the historically affordable range of 2.5-3.5. Housing starts averaged ~1.4 million/year from 2010-2023, compared to ~1.7 million/year needed to keep pace with household formation and replacement. The construction labor force is approximately 400,000 workers short of pre-2008 levels relative to population growth. These are the measurable quantities: a deficit in units, a gap in production rate, and a constrained labor supply.

The principal contradiction is between the need for new housing (driven by population growth, household formation, and depreciation of existing stock) and the regulatory barriers that prevent construction where demand is highest. Zoning restrictions represent the core material constraint. In cities like San Francisco, approximately 38% of residentially zoned land permits only single-family homes. In Los Angeles, single-family zoning covers ~75% of residential land. The dependency chain is: housing affordability requires adequate supply → adequate supply requires construction → construction requires permittable land → permittable land requires zoning that permits multi-family housing at sufficient density. When this chain breaks at the zoning link, no amount of demand-side intervention (subsidies, rent control, down-payment assistance) can produce additional units, because the material condition for building (legal permission to build) is absent.

The international evidence reinforces this diagnosis. Tokyo, which reformed its zoning code in 2002 to make most residential zones "by right" for buildings up to ~10 stories, built 142,417 housing units in 2023 in a metro area of 14 million, more than the entire state of California (population 39 million). Tokyo's rent growth has been approximately flat in real terms for two decades. The material condition that changed was the regulatory permission to build. The result was a change in the price condition. This is resolution by changing conditions, not redistribution within unchanged conditions.

### Skeptic

The Defender grounds the argument in a genuine housing deficit, which the Skeptic does not dispute: underproduction relative to demand is real. The question is whether "primarily a supply problem" correctly identifies the principal contradiction, or whether it mistakes a symptom for a cause.

The material conditions the Defender omits: (1) Institutional investors purchased approximately 26% of single-family homes sold in 2021-2022 (MetLife Investment Management data), removing units from the owner-occupied market and converting them to rental properties at higher effective cost. (2) Short-term rental platforms (Airbnb, VRBO) have removed an estimated 1-1.5 million units from the long-term housing market in the US. (3) Vacant housing units in the US total approximately 15 million (Census Bureau 2022), of which ~10 million are classified as "other vacant" (not for sale, not for rent, not seasonal). The Defender's framing treats housing as a production problem, but these conditions reveal a distribution and financialization problem: units exist but are withheld from the market, allocated to investment returns rather than habitation, or priced above the median household's ability to pay.

The contradiction the Defender misses is internal to the supply argument itself. New construction in high-demand markets tends toward luxury units because developers maximize return on scarce permittable land. In New York City, 80% of new units completed in 2019-2023 were market-rate luxury (NYU Furman Center). Building more luxury units does not resolve the affordability crisis for households earning $40,000-80,000/year. The dependency chain the Defender traces (zoning reform → more construction → lower prices) has a broken link: more construction does not necessarily mean more affordable construction. The market produces what maximizes profit, and absent intervention, that is luxury housing. The supply argument requires a sub-argument about filtering (luxury units free up mid-tier units, which free up affordable units), but filtering operates on decadal timescales and assumes a chain of moves that empirical evidence shows is incomplete in tight markets (Rosenthal 2014, Mast 2021).

The Tokyo comparison omits critical material conditions. Japan's population has been declining since 2008 (from 128 million to ~125 million in 2023). Japan has no net immigration pressure on housing. Land values in Tokyo declined for most of the 1990s-2010s after the asset bubble collapse. The Defender attributes Tokyo's stable rents to zoning reform, but the counterfactual is a shrinking-population country with deflated land values. The material conditions of Tokyo and San Francisco are not comparable in the dimensions that determine housing pressure.

## Round 2

### Defender

The Skeptic raises three counter-conditions: investor purchases, short-term rentals, and vacant units. Each must be tested against the dependency structure of the supply argument.

Investor purchases: institutional investors bought 26% of single-family homes at peak in 2021-2022, but this figure has since fallen to ~15-18% as interest rates rose (CoreLogic 2023). The dependency question is: why are investors able to profit from housing? Because housing is scarce relative to demand, which supports price appreciation and rental yield. If supply were abundant, the return on housing investment would fall, reducing institutional interest. The contradiction runs in the opposite direction from the Skeptic's framing: investor demand is a consequence of undersupply, not a cause. In markets with elastic supply (Houston, which has no zoning code), institutional investors are present but prices remain moderate (median home price ~$330,000 vs. San Francisco's ~$1.3 million) because supply responds to demand.

Short-term rentals: the estimated 1-1.5 million Airbnb units represent ~1% of the 140 million US housing units. Research by Barron, Kung, and Proserpio (2021) found that a 1% increase in Airbnb listings raises rents by 0.018% and house prices by 0.026%. These effects are measurable but small relative to the 5.5-6.8 million unit deficit. The Skeptic identifies a real friction but overstates its causal weight. If the deficit were 1 million units instead of 6 million, Airbnb conversions would be proportionally more significant. The scale of the shortage determines whether marginal demand-side factors are primary or secondary.

Vacant units: the 10 million "other vacant" units are disproportionately located in declining markets (Detroit, Cleveland, rural counties) where demand is low. The vacancy rate in high-demand metros is ~3-5%, well below the ~7-8% considered a balanced market. The material conditions of vacancy do not match the geography of the crisis. Units exist where people do not want to live; units are absent where people do. This is a supply problem, not a distribution problem, because the "solution" of redistributing people to vacant units requires people to move away from employment, which breaks the dependency between housing and livelihood.

The Skeptic's strongest point is the luxury-construction objection. The filtering literature does show that new market-rate construction reduces displacement and lowers rents in nearby areas (Mast 2021 finds each 100 new market-rate units create a chain of moves that frees ~70 units in below-median-income tracts). The effect is real but slow. However, this is an argument for building more, not less: if filtering is slow, the solution is to increase the volume so that the filtering effect operates across more units simultaneously. The constraint on building non-luxury units is, again, zoning: inclusionary zoning mandates (requiring 10-20% affordable units) work only when overall construction volume is high enough to absorb the cross-subsidy. When zoning restricts total volume, the affordable mandate applies to a shrinking denominator.

### Skeptic

The Defender's rebuttal on investor purchases, by tracing the dependency from scarcity to investor profitability, is the strongest argument in this debate. If housing were abundant, investment returns would decline and institutional capital would flow elsewhere. This dependency chain is logically sound and the Skeptic concedes the point as a structural matter: investor demand amplifies the crisis but does not originate it.

However, the Defender's framework, correct about the direction of causality, understates a feedback loop that converts a supply problem into something more stubborn. The contradiction is: undersupply raises prices → rising prices attract speculative capital → speculative capital raises prices further → higher prices justify restrictive lending and NIMBYism (homeowners protect asset values) → NIMBYism blocks supply. This is a self-reinforcing cycle, and identifying supply as the "primary" cause, while accurate at origin, obscures the fact that the system has acquired additional lock-in mechanisms that a supply-only intervention will not fully address. Breaking the cycle requires changing multiple conditions simultaneously: zoning reform (supply), anti-speculation measures (demand), and public housing production (non-market supply).

The Defender's Houston example reveals a contradiction the Defender does not acknowledge. Houston has no zoning code and relatively elastic supply, yet it has significant housing quality problems: poor building standards, flood-zone construction (exacerbated by no land-use regulation), long commutes due to sprawl, and an affordable housing stock that is physically deteriorating. The material condition the Houston model produces is cheap housing at the cost of externalities: flood risk, car dependency, infrastructure burden, and environmental damage. If "solving" the supply problem requires accepting these conditions, the resolution has not changed the underlying contradictions but displaced them from price to quality, safety, and sustainability.

On filtering: the Defender cites Mast (2021) correctly. The Skeptic does not dispute that new construction generates moves that benefit lower-income households. The dependency, however, runs through time. Mast's data shows the chain of moves operating over 1-5 years in expanding markets. In the highest-demand markets (San Francisco, New York, Boston), population inflow can absorb new supply before filtering reaches lower-income quintiles. The condition that must change for filtering to work is not just "build more" but "build more than demand growth," and in the most constrained markets, demand growth is driven by job creation in high-wage industries (tech, finance, biotech) whose employees outbid existing residents. The supply argument is not wrong; it is incomplete without an account of the income inequality that shapes demand.

## Round 3

### Defender

The Skeptic's feedback-loop argument is the most sophisticated challenge in this debate. The claim is that supply shortage → price appreciation → speculative capital → NIMBYism → further supply restriction forms a self-reinforcing cycle requiring multi-causal intervention. The Defender does not deny the feedback loop; the question is where intervention most effectively breaks it.

Consider the dependency structure: every element in the cycle depends on the scarcity condition. Remove scarcity, and the downstream effects attenuate. Speculative capital exits when returns decline. Homeowner opposition loses its economic rationale when prices stop appreciating. Restrictive lending eases when collateral values stabilize. The supply link is the load-bearing condition on which the entire cycle rests. This is not to say other interventions are useless; anti-speculation measures (vacancy taxes, Airbnb restrictions) and public housing can complement supply reform. But they cannot substitute for it. Demand-side interventions in the absence of supply reform are the definition of redistributing effort within unchanged conditions: rent control allocates existing units differently without creating new ones; subsidies raise effective demand without raising effective supply, thereby pushing prices higher.

The Houston objection is instructive precisely because it shows what happens when supply is elastic but other conditions remain unchanged. The Defender agrees that Houston's model has externalities. But these are conditions of Houston's specific regulatory approach (no zoning of any kind, weak building codes, no flood-plain restrictions), not inherent consequences of housing supply elasticity. Japan, Germany, and the Netherlands combine liberal building permissions with strong building codes, infrastructure planning, and environmental regulation. The resolution is not "Houston or San Francisco" but "zoning reform within a framework of building quality and land-use planning." The Skeptic's argument that supply elasticity necessarily produces Houston-style externalities treats Houston's specific conditions as universal, which they are not.

On filtering and income inequality: the Defender concedes that income inequality shapes the demand curve. In markets where high-wage job creation outpaces housing production, filtering is slower and less complete. But this reinforces rather than undermines the supply argument. If demand is growing fast, the solution is more supply, faster. The alternative the Skeptic implies, constraining demand through restricting job creation or high-wage employment, is not a housing policy but an economic policy that reduces total welfare. The material conditions that must change are the conditions of production (zoning, permitting, labor supply, construction technology), not the conditions of demand (employment, wages, migration). Modular construction, factory-built housing, and reformed permitting timelines (Austin's SB 2 reform reduced permitting from 6-12 months to weeks) change the rate of production. These are supply-side changes to material conditions.

### Skeptic

The Defender's final argument is structurally sound: the feedback loop depends on the scarcity condition, and removing scarcity weakens every downstream mechanism. The Skeptic does not rebut this logic. The dispute is about whether "primarily a supply problem" adequately describes a system with multiple lock-in mechanisms, even if supply is the originating cause.

The Defender's proposed resolution (zoning reform + building codes + infrastructure planning) is more nuanced than "just build more," and the Skeptic concedes this is a workable framework. The gap between this position and the Skeptic's is narrower than it appeared in Round 1. The remaining disagreement is about emphasis: the Defender argues that supply reform is necessary and sufficient (with quality regulation as a complement); the Skeptic argues that supply reform is necessary but insufficient without concurrent anti-financialization measures and income-targeted programs.

The contradictions that remain unresolved: (1) Zoning reform requires political action by homeowners whose wealth depends on scarcity. The principal agents of change are the principal beneficiaries of the status quo. This is the deepest structural contradiction in housing policy, and neither side has proposed a material condition change that resolves it. Historical precedent (Japan 2002, Oregon 2019 HB 2001, Minneapolis 2040 plan) shows it is possible but requires sustained political pressure from non-homeowner constituencies. (2) Construction labor is constrained. The US needs approximately 400,000 additional construction workers to meet projected demand. Immigration reform and workforce training are supply-side interventions, but they depend on political conditions that are currently hostile to expanded immigration. (3) The "best" supply reform (upzoning near transit) conflicts with existing residents' preferences in precisely the neighborhoods where density is most efficient. This contradiction between aggregate welfare and individual incumbent interest is not resolved by the supply framework; it is the condition that blocks the supply framework.

The Skeptic's position, refined through three rounds: the housing crisis is primarily a supply problem in its origins and its most effective resolution. But "primarily" obscures the degree to which secondary mechanisms (financialization, income inequality, political lock-in by homeowners) have become load-bearing features of the crisis that will not self-correct through supply alone. The most honest framing is: supply reform is the necessary condition for resolution, but it is not the sufficient condition.

## Verdict

**Summary of strongest arguments (grounded in material conditions):**

The Defender's strongest argument was the dependency-chain analysis: housing affordability depends on adequate supply, supply depends on construction, construction depends on legal permission to build, and that permission is blocked by zoning restrictions in the highest-demand markets. The 5.5-6.8 million unit deficit, the 38-75% single-family-only zoning rates in major cities, and the Houston/Tokyo comparisons grounded this chain in measurable conditions. The Defender's rebuttal of investor purchases (tracing investor profitability to scarcity rather than the reverse) was logically decisive.

The Skeptic's strongest argument was the feedback-loop analysis: undersupply creates a self-reinforcing cycle (scarcity → appreciation → speculation → NIMBYism → further restriction) that has acquired its own lock-in dynamics. The luxury-construction objection and the filtering timeline forced the Defender to refine the supply argument beyond simple "build more." The political-economy observation (homeowners as both beneficiaries of scarcity and gatekeepers of reform) identified a genuine structural contradiction the Defender acknowledged but did not resolve.

**Key contradictions identified:**

- Defender: need for housing vs. regulatory barriers to construction; demand-side interventions vs. supply-side constraints (redistribution within unchanged conditions); filtering as slow but real.
- Skeptic: supply as origin vs. supply as insufficient remedy; investor demand as amplifier not cause (conceded); zoning reform as dependent on consent of those who benefit from the status quo.

**Unresolved dependencies and conditions for resolution:**

1. Political economy of zoning reform: requires either (a) state-level preemption of local zoning (Oregon, California SB 9/SB 10 model), (b) sustained pressure from renter/non-homeowner coalitions, or (c) fiscal crises that force municipalities to permit development.
2. Construction labor supply: requires immigration reform and/or workforce training investment, both politically constrained.
3. Anti-financialization measures: vacancy taxes, Airbnb restrictions, and limits on institutional purchases may be necessary complements but have uncertain efficacy at scale.

**Assessment: 7/10 confidence in the proposition.** The debate demonstrates that the housing crisis is primarily a supply problem in the sense that supply constraint is the necessary and originating cause, and supply reform is the most effective lever for resolution. However, the Skeptic successfully argued that "primarily" should not be read as "exclusively" or "sufficiently." The feedback mechanisms, political lock-in, and filtering limitations mean supply reform must be accompanied by complementary interventions to achieve affordability across income levels. The material conditions favor the Defender's core claim while validating the Skeptic's call for a multi-instrument approach.
