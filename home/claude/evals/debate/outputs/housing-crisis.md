# The housing crisis is primarily a supply problem

## Round 1

### Defender

The material conditions of US housing: the National Association of Realtors estimates a deficit of 5.5-6.8 million units as of 2023. Median existing home price reached $389,800 (NAR 2023) against median household income of $80,610 (Census Bureau), yielding a price-to-income ratio of ~4.8 — well above the historically affordable 2.5-3.5 range. Housing starts averaged ~1.4 million/year from 2010-2023 versus ~1.7 million/year needed for household formation and stock replacement. The construction workforce is ~400,000 workers short of pre-2008 levels relative to population growth.

The principal contradiction is between the need for new housing (driven by population growth, household formation, depreciation) and regulatory barriers that prevent construction where demand is highest. Zoning is the binding material constraint: San Francisco zones ~38% of residential land for single-family only; Los Angeles, ~75%. The dependency chain: housing affordability requires adequate supply → supply requires construction → construction requires permittable land → permittable land requires zoning allowing multi-family density. When this chain breaks at the zoning link, no demand-side intervention (subsidies, rent control, down-payment assistance) produces additional units, because the material condition for building — legal permission — is absent.

International evidence reinforces this. Tokyo reformed its zoning code in 2002 to permit by-right construction up to ~10 stories in most residential zones. Tokyo built 142,417 units in 2023 (metro pop. 14 million), more than all of California (pop. 39 million). Tokyo's real rents have been approximately flat for two decades. The material condition that changed was regulatory permission. The result was a change in price conditions. This is resolution by changing conditions, not redistribution within unchanged conditions.

### Skeptic

The Defender grounds the argument in a genuine housing deficit, which the Skeptic does not dispute: underproduction relative to demand is real. The question is whether "primarily a supply problem" correctly identifies the principal contradiction or mistakes a symptom for a cause.

Material conditions the Defender omits: (1) Institutional investors purchased ~26% of single-family homes in 2021-2022 (MetLife Investment Management), removing units from owner-occupancy and converting them to rental at higher effective cost. (2) Short-term rental platforms (Airbnb, VRBO) have removed an estimated 1-1.5 million units from long-term housing markets. (3) The Census Bureau counts ~15 million vacant housing units, of which ~10 million are classified "other vacant" — not for sale, not for rent, not seasonal. The Defender treats housing as a production problem, but these conditions reveal a distribution and financialization problem: units exist but are withheld, allocated to investment returns rather than habitation, or priced beyond median affordability.

The contradiction internal to the supply argument: new construction in high-demand markets tilts toward luxury because developers maximize return on scarce permittable land. In NYC, 80% of new units completed 2019-2023 were market-rate luxury (NYU Furman Center). Building more does not mean building affordably. The dependency chain (zoning reform → more construction → lower prices) has a broken link: the market produces what maximizes profit, and absent intervention, that is luxury housing. The filtering mechanism (luxury frees mid-tier, which frees affordable) operates on decadal timescales and is incomplete in tight markets (Rosenthal 2014, Mast 2021).

The Tokyo comparison omits critical conditions. Japan's population has declined since 2008 (128M → ~125M). Japan has no net immigration pressure. Tokyo land values deflated after the asset bubble. Attributing stable rents solely to zoning reform ignores a shrinking-population, deflated-land-value context. The material conditions of Tokyo and San Francisco are not comparable on the dimensions that determine housing pressure.

## Round 2

### Defender

The Skeptic raises three counter-conditions: investor purchases, short-term rentals, and vacant units. Each must be tested against the supply framework's dependency structure.

Investor purchases: institutions bought 26% of homes at 2021-2022 peak, since fallen to ~15-18% as rates rose (CoreLogic 2023). The dependency question: why can investors profit from housing? Because scarcity relative to demand supports price appreciation and rental yield. If supply were abundant, returns would decline and institutional capital would exit. In Houston — no zoning code, elastic supply — institutional investors are present but prices remain moderate (~$330,000 median vs. San Francisco's ~$1.3M) because supply responds to demand. Investor demand is a consequence of undersupply, not a cause. The causality runs opposite to the Skeptic's framing.

Short-term rentals: ~1-1.5 million Airbnb units represent ~1% of 140 million US housing units. Barron, Kung & Proserpio (2021) found a 1% Airbnb listing increase raises rents 0.018% and prices 0.026%. Measurable but small relative to a 5.5-6.8M unit deficit. A real friction, but secondary in causal weight.

Vacant units: the 10 million "other vacant" are disproportionately in declining markets (Detroit, Cleveland, rural counties). Vacancy rates in high-demand metros are 3-5%, below the 7-8% balanced-market threshold. Units exist where people don't want to live; they're absent where people do. "Redistributing" people to vacant units requires moving them from employment — breaking the housing-livelihood dependency.

The Skeptic's strongest point is luxury construction. The filtering literature shows new market-rate construction does reduce displacement: Mast (2021) finds each 100 new market-rate units create a move chain freeing ~70 units in below-median-income tracts. The effect is real but slow. This argues for more volume, not less: if filtering is slow, increase the denominator. Inclusionary zoning mandates (10-20% affordable) work only when total volume is high enough to absorb the cross-subsidy. Restricted total volume means the affordable mandate applies to a shrinking base.

### Skeptic

The Defender's rebuttal on investor purchases — tracing profitability to scarcity — is the strongest argument in this debate. If housing were abundant, investment returns decline and capital flows elsewhere. The Skeptic concedes: investor demand amplifies the crisis but does not originate it. The dependency direction is as the Defender states.

However, the Defender's framework understates a feedback loop. The contradiction: undersupply raises prices → rising prices attract speculative capital → speculation raises prices further → higher prices justify NIMBYism (homeowners protecting asset values) → NIMBYism blocks supply → undersupply persists. This self-reinforcing cycle means that identifying supply as the originating cause, while accurate, obscures lock-in mechanisms that supply-only intervention will not fully address. Breaking the cycle requires changing multiple conditions simultaneously: zoning reform (supply), anti-speculation measures (demand), and public housing production (non-market supply).

The Houston example reveals a contradiction the Defender doesn't acknowledge. Houston has elastic supply and moderate prices, but also: poor building standards, flood-zone construction (no land-use regulation), long commutes from sprawl, and deteriorating affordable stock. The material conditions Houston produces are cheap housing at the cost of externalities — flood risk, car dependency, infrastructure burden. If "solving" supply requires accepting these conditions, the resolution displaces contradictions from price to quality, safety, and sustainability rather than resolving them.

On filtering: Mast (2021) is correctly cited and the Skeptic doesn't dispute the mechanism. The dependency runs through time: in the highest-demand markets (SF, NYC, Boston), population inflow from high-wage industries (tech, finance, biotech) can absorb new supply before filtering reaches lower quintiles. The condition for filtering to work is not just "build more" but "build more than demand growth." The supply argument is not wrong — it is incomplete without accounting for income inequality shaping the demand curve.

## Round 3

### Defender

The Skeptic's feedback-loop argument is the most sophisticated challenge in this debate. The claim: supply shortage → price appreciation → speculative capital → NIMBYism → further restriction forms a self-reinforcing cycle requiring multi-causal intervention.

The Defender does not deny the feedback loop. The question is where intervention most effectively breaks it. Consider the dependency structure: every element depends on the scarcity condition. Remove scarcity, and downstream effects attenuate. Speculative capital exits when returns decline. Homeowner opposition loses its economic rationale when prices stop appreciating. Restrictive lending eases when collateral values stabilize. Supply is the load-bearing condition. This does not make other interventions useless — vacancy taxes, Airbnb restrictions, and public housing complement supply reform. But they cannot substitute for it. Rent control allocates existing units without creating new ones. Subsidies raise effective demand without raising supply, pushing prices higher. These are redistribution within unchanged conditions.

The Houston objection is instructive. The Defender agrees Houston has externalities from its specific approach (no zoning at all, weak building codes, no flood-plain rules). But these are conditions of Houston's regulatory model, not inherent consequences of supply elasticity. Japan, Germany, and the Netherlands combine liberal building permissions with strong building codes, infrastructure planning, and environmental regulation. The resolution is zoning reform within a quality framework — not "Houston or San Francisco."

On filtering and income inequality: the Defender concedes income inequality shapes demand. In markets where high-wage job creation outpaces housing production, filtering is slower. But this reinforces the supply argument: if demand grows fast, the answer is more supply, faster. The alternative — constraining demand by restricting employment or wages — reduces total welfare. The material conditions to change are conditions of production: zoning, permitting, labor supply, construction technology. Modular construction, factory-built housing, and reformed permitting (Austin's SB 2 reform cut timelines from 6-12 months to weeks) change the production rate. These are supply-side changes to material conditions.

### Skeptic

The Defender's final argument is structurally sound: the feedback loop depends on scarcity, and removing scarcity weakens every downstream mechanism. The Skeptic does not rebut this logic.

The Defender's proposed resolution — zoning reform + building codes + infrastructure planning — is more nuanced than "just build more," and the Skeptic concedes this as a workable framework. The gap between positions is narrower than Round 1 suggested. The remaining disagreement is emphasis: the Defender argues supply reform is necessary and sufficient (with quality regulation as complement); the Skeptic argues it is necessary but insufficient without concurrent anti-financialization and income-targeted measures.

Contradictions that remain unresolved: (1) Zoning reform requires political action by homeowners whose wealth depends on scarcity. The principal agents of change are the principal beneficiaries of the status quo. This is the deepest structural contradiction, and neither side resolved it. Historical precedent (Japan 2002, Oregon HB 2001 2019, Minneapolis 2040) shows reform is possible but requires sustained pressure from non-homeowner constituencies. (2) Construction labor is constrained by ~400,000 workers. Immigration reform and workforce training are supply-side interventions dependent on hostile political conditions. (3) Optimal supply reform (upzoning near transit) conflicts with incumbent residents' preferences precisely where density is most efficient — a contradiction between aggregate welfare and individual interest that the supply framework identifies but does not resolve.

The Skeptic's refined position: the housing crisis is primarily a supply problem in its origins and most effective resolution path. "Primarily" should not be read as "exclusively" or "sufficiently." Secondary mechanisms — financialization, income inequality, homeowner political lock-in — have become load-bearing features that will not self-correct through supply alone. Supply reform is the necessary condition for resolution, but not the sufficient condition.

## Verdict

**Strongest arguments (grounded in material conditions):**

Defender: The dependency-chain analysis (affordability → supply → construction → zoning permission) grounded in the 5.5-6.8M unit deficit, 38-75% single-family zoning rates, and the Houston/Tokyo comparisons. The rebuttal of investor purchases — tracing profitability to scarcity rather than the reverse — was logically decisive.

Skeptic: The feedback-loop analysis (scarcity → appreciation → speculation → NIMBYism → restriction) identifying self-reinforcing dynamics. The luxury-construction objection and filtering timeline forced refinement of the supply argument. The political-economy observation (homeowners as both scarcity beneficiaries and reform gatekeepers) identified a structural contradiction the Defender acknowledged but did not resolve.

**Key contradictions:**

1. Need for housing vs. regulatory barriers to construction (Defender)
2. Demand-side interventions vs. supply constraints — redistribution within unchanged conditions (Defender)
3. Supply as originating cause vs. supply as insufficient remedy due to lock-in mechanisms (Skeptic)
4. Zoning reform dependent on consent of those who benefit from scarcity (Skeptic)

**Unresolved dependencies:**

- Political economy of zoning reform: requires state-level preemption, renter coalition pressure, or fiscal crisis
- Construction labor: requires immigration reform and/or workforce training, both politically constrained
- Anti-financialization measures: vacancy taxes, STR restrictions, institutional purchase limits — uncertain efficacy at scale

**Assessment:** The Defender holds the stronger position. The supply constraint is the originating and load-bearing cause of the crisis, and supply reform is the most effective lever. The Skeptic successfully argued that "primarily" should not mean "exclusively" — secondary mechanisms require complementary intervention. The material conditions favor the proposition while validating the Skeptic's insistence on multi-instrument approach. Confidence: 7/10.
