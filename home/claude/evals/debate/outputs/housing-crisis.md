# Debate: "The housing crisis is primarily a supply problem"

**Defender:** Argues FOR — the housing crisis is primarily a supply problem. Labels: [CONDITIONS] [CONTRADICTION] [DEPENDENCY] [RESOLUTION]. Every claim cites measurable data. Resolutions change material conditions, not effort.
**Skeptic:** Argues AGAINST — supply is insufficient as a primary explanation; demand-composition, financialization, and allocation distortions are co-primary. Same labels. Must propose alternative condition-changes, not just critique.

---

## Round 1

### Defender

[CONDITIONS] The U.S. housing supply gap reached 4.03 million homes in 2025, up from 3.8 million in 2024 ([Realtor.com via PR Newswire](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/housing-supply-gap-surpasses-4-million-homes-in-2025-as-construction-fails-to-keep-pace-with-demand-302701775.html)). In 2025, 1.41 million new households formed against only 1.36 million housing starts, producing a net deficit of ~50,000 units in a single year ([Realtor.com via Yield PRO](https://yieldpro.com/2026/03/housing-supply-gap-surpasses-4-million-homes-in-2025/)). The homeowner vacancy rate fell to 1.0% in 2024, the lowest in Census Bureau recorded history ([FRED](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/USHVAC)). Define "housing crisis" operationally: median home price-to-income ratio of 5.0 in 2025, versus the historically recommended ratio of 2.6 ([Visual Capitalist](https://www.visualcapitalist.com/charted-american-income-vs-home-prices-1985-2025/)).

[DEPENDENCY] Price is a function of supply relative to demand. If supply < demand, prices rise. If vacancy rates are at historic lows (1.0% homeowner, 7.2% rental in Q4 2025 per [Census Bureau](https://www.census.gov/housing/hvs/current/index.html)), then the binding constraint is physical units, not purchasing power. The chain runs: regulatory barriers -> constrained land use -> insufficient starts -> widening deficit -> price inflation. All demand-side interventions (subsidies, rate cuts) without new units redistribute access to a fixed stock while pushing prices higher.

[CONTRADICTION] I acknowledge a structural tension in my own position: even if supply is the primary driver, the construction industry cannot scale to close a 4-million-unit gap under current conditions. At 2025's pace, even a 50% increase in housing starts would require seven years to eliminate the deficit ([Realtor.com](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/housing-supply-gap-surpasses-4-million-homes-in-2025-as-construction-fails-to-keep-pace-with-demand-302701775.html)). The construction labor shortage stands at 32% across residential trades, and building material costs rose 41.6% since the pandemic ([NAHB via NAHREP](https://nahrep.org/housinghub/2025/10/21/building-barriers-how-rising-construction-costs-impact-the-housing-affordability-crisis/)). Identifying supply as the primary problem and achieving supply as the solution structurally undermine each other: resolving the bottleneck requires the very construction capacity that is itself constrained.

[RESOLUTION] Condition-change: eliminate single-family-only zoning in metro areas above 100,000 population. Evidence: Minneapolis's 2040 Plan, which ended single-family zoning, produced home prices 16-34% lower and rents 17-34% lower than counterfactual over five years ([Gu & Munro, SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5347083)). This changes a regulatory condition rather than redistributing effort within unchanged conditions. Measurable target: reduce median price-to-income ratio from 5.0 toward 3.5 within a decade.

### Skeptic

[CONDITIONS] There are 15.1 million vacant housing units in the United States as of 2024, representing 10.3% of total housing stock ([Census Bureau via USAFacts](https://usafacts.org/articles/how-many-vacant-homes-are-there-in-the-us/)). Investors purchased 30% of all single-family homes in 2025, up from 29% in 2024 ([HousingWire](https://www.housingwire.com/articles/investor-share-home-purchases-2025/)). Home prices rose 54% over five years while wages rose only 29% ([Visual Capitalist](https://www.visualcapitalist.com/why-u-s-homes-feel-pricier-house-prices-vs-income-1985-2025/)). These quantities define a demand-distortion problem layered on top of a wage-stagnation problem, not a pure supply deficit.

[DEPENDENCY] The Defender traces: regulatory barriers -> constrained land use -> insufficient starts -> price inflation. But this chain has a broken link at the terminus. 15.1 million units sit vacant. If the gross vacancy rate is 10.3% while the "supply gap" is 4 million units, then the number of vacant units exceeds the gap by 3.8x. The binding constraint is not the physical existence of units but their allocation: who holds them, at what price, and for what purpose. Investor purchases at 30% of transactions represent demand that does not correspond to occupancy need, inflating prices independent of unit count. The chain should read: new supply -> investor absorption -> no price relief for median buyers.

[CONTRADICTION] I acknowledge that not all 15.1 million vacancies are usable: many are seasonal, in-transition, or in locations mismatched to demand. The Census categories include units "held off market" for various reasons, with over 5.8 million classified as seasonal or occasional use. So the condition (large vacancy stock exists) and the claim (this stock could house people) structurally undermine each other because much of that stock is geographically or functionally incompatible with where demand concentrates. My own strongest argument contains a distributional flaw that reduces 15.1M to a much smaller actionable number.

[RESOLUTION] Condition-change: implement a graduated vacancy tax on units held vacant for >12 months in metro areas where rental vacancy < 5%, combined with a cap on investor share of purchases in these same markets to 15% of annual transactions. This changes the allocation condition rather than the total unit count. Measurable outcome: reduce investor purchase share from 30% to 15%, return an estimated 500,000-1,000,000 functionally vacant units to occupancy within 3 years, targeting a rental vacancy rate increase from 7.2% to 9-10% nationally.

## Round 2

### Defender

**Response to Skeptic's strongest point: 15.1 million vacant units dwarf the 4 million gap, suggesting allocation, not supply, is the binding constraint.**

[CONDITIONS] Of those 15.1 million vacant units, the Census Bureau classifies roughly 5.8 million as seasonal/occasional use and 2.1 million as "held off market" for other reasons ([Census Bureau HVS](https://www.census.gov/housing/hvs/current/index.html)). The Skeptic acknowledged this weakness. New data: institutional investors (those owning 100+ homes) hold only 3% of the single-family rental stock nationally ([Brookings via EconFact](https://econofact.org/factbrief/do-private-equity-firms-own-20-of-single-family-homes)). The "30% investor share" is dominated by small investors (1-10 properties) who constitute 91% of investor-owned homes ([BatchData](https://batchdata.io/blog/real-estate-investor-activity-nationwide)). These small investors typically provide rental housing, converting owner-occupied stock to renter-occupied stock rather than removing it from the housing market.

[DEPENDENCY] The Skeptic's chain runs: investor purchases (A) -> reduced owner-occupant access (B) -> higher prices (C). The link A->B is broken for the majority of transactions. Small landlords (91% of investor activity) add rental supply. Institutional investors are now net sellers, having sold more homes than they purchased for six consecutive quarters ([BatchData](https://batchdata.io/blog/real-estate-investor-activity-nationwide)). The Skeptic's proposed 15% cap on investor transactions would primarily restrict small landlords who supply rental units, potentially worsening the rental shortage. The fastest-growing segment is medium-sized investors (10-99 properties), whose share grew from 6% to 10% between June 2024 and June 2025, but this is a 4-percentage-point shift in a 30% share: it cannot explain a 4-million-unit deficit.

[CONTRADICTION] My own position faces a new internal tension: zoning reform, which I proposed in Round 1, operates on a 5-10 year timeline, but the construction labor shortage (32% deficit, adding 1.98 months to average project completion, suppressing starts by 18% nationally per [HBI/NAHB](https://www.nahb.org/blog/2025/10/hbi-labor-market-report)) means that even permissive zoning cannot produce units without workers to build them. The condition I propose changing (zoning) and the condition required for that change to produce results (labor supply) structurally undermine each other: liberalized zoning without labor produces permits, not housing.

[RESOLUTION] Condition-change: pair zoning reform with construction workforce expansion. Immigrant workers already comprise 25.5% of the construction workforce and one-third of construction tradespeople ([HBI](https://hbi.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/Fall-2025-Final-Construction-Labor-Market-Report-Update.pdf)). Expand visa allocations for construction trades by 100,000 annually. Measurable outcome: reduce average project completion from 11 months back toward 7 months, enabling an additional ~50,000 units/year within 2 years of implementation. This addresses the broken dependency between zoning permission and actual construction.

### Skeptic

**Response to Defender's strongest point: institutional investors are only 3% of rental stock, and small investors provide rental supply rather than removing housing.**

[CONDITIONS] While small investors dominate the category, medium-sized investors (10-99 properties) grew their market share from 6% to 10% between June 2024 and June 2025 ([BatchData](https://batchdata.io/blog/real-estate-investor-activity-nationwide)). This is the fastest-growing segment and represents professionalized capital, not mom-and-pop landlords. The zero-interest-rate era of the 2010s drove financial engineering over physical development: capital that could have funded construction flowed into acquisition of existing stock ([CBRE Investment Management](https://www.cbreim.com/insights/articles/digging-out-of-the-us-housing-affordability-crisis)). Between 2019 and 2022, home prices rose 43% while incomes rose only 7% ([Kinder Institute, Rice University](https://kinder.rice.edu/urbanedge/gap-between-income-growth-and-housing-cost-increases-continues-grow)). A price acceleration of this magnitude far outstrips what a 50,000-unit/year shortfall would predict in a $44 trillion housing market. Demand-side forces amplify price signals beyond what supply gaps alone explain.

[DEPENDENCY] The Defender traces: zoning reform (A) -> more permits (B) -> more units (C) -> lower prices (D). I accept A->B. But B->C is broken by construction costs: materials costs rose 41.6% post-pandemic, and tariffs alone add $10,900 per single-family home ([NAHB via NAHREP](https://nahrep.org/housinghub/2025/10/21/building-barriers-how-rising-construction-costs-impact-the-housing-affordability-crisis/)). Even with labor and zoning solved, C->D is questionable: new construction at $200-350/sq ft cannot produce units affordable to median-income households without subsidy. The Minneapolis results (16-34% lower prices) occurred in a market with specific conditions: relatively low land costs and existing infrastructure that may not transfer to coastal metros where the crisis is most acute.

[CONTRADICTION] My demand-side framing faces a structural tension I must flag: if investor caps and vacancy taxes successfully reduced prices by restricting demand, this would simultaneously reduce the profitability of new construction, discouraging the very supply additions I concede are needed long-term. The condition I want to change (investor-driven price inflation) and the condition the market needs (profit incentive for builders) structurally undermine each other. Demand suppression in a supply-constrained market risks freezing construction rather than enabling affordability.

[RESOLUTION] Condition-change: rather than blunt investor caps, implement a split tax regime. Tax gains on existing-home acquisitions at a higher rate while offering accelerated depreciation for investors who fund new construction. This redirects capital from acquisition to production. European research confirms that without demand-side channeling, new supply becomes "additional investment objects rather than genuinely affordable homes" ([Tandfonline, 2026](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/2833115X.2026.2629895)). Measurable outcome: shift 20% of investor capital from acquisitions to new-build, producing an estimated 60,000-80,000 additional units/year within 3 years. Target: reduce median price-to-income from 5.0 to 4.2 within 5 years.

## Round 3

*No new arguments. New evidence allowed. Final resolutions.*

### Defender

[CONDITIONS] The 2026 base-case forecast projects home prices rising only 0.5% nationally, with incomes finally growing faster than prices for the first time in years ([Fortune](https://fortune.com/2025/12/14/housing-market-outlook-2026-new-era-affordability-home-prices-incomes/)). The NAHB Housing Opportunity Index showed mild affordability gains in H2 2025 ([NAHB](https://www.nahb.org/news-and-economics/press-releases/2026/03/affordability-posts-mild-gains-in-second-half-of-2025-but-crisis-continues)). NLIHC's Gap 2026 report identifies a shortage of 7.1 million homes affordable and available to extremely low-income renters ([NLIHC](https://nlihc.org/news/nlihc-releases-gap-2026-shortage-affordable-homes)). This last figure is the most telling: even if investor behavior and allocation were optimized, 7.1 million affordable units do not physically exist at the price point needed. No reallocation of existing stock addresses a gap of that magnitude.

[DEPENDENCY] The full causal chain, incorporating both sides' contributions across three rounds: state-level zoning reform (A) -> workforce expansion via visa reform (B) -> permits convert to starts (C) -> material cost reduction via tariff reform (D) -> units complete at accessible price points (E) -> vacancy rates normalize (F) -> price-to-income ratio falls (G). Each link requires the prior. The Skeptic's split-tax proposal from Round 2 fits between A and B as a capital-direction mechanism. I accept it as complementary. But the chain's foundation remains physical unit production. Demand-side measures without A through D produce zero new units.

[CONTRADICTION] Supply-side primacy and affordability for the most vulnerable structurally undermine each other. New market-rate construction at current costs ($200-350/sq ft) cannot serve the bottom 30% of the income distribution without subsidy. The very population most affected by the crisis (extremely low-income renters facing a 7.1M unit gap per NLIHC) cannot be served by the market mechanism I advocate. Supply is the primary problem, but market-rate supply is not the primary solution for the most vulnerable segment. I accept this as an unresolved structural tension in my position: the problem is supply, but the fix for the worst-affected requires public production outside market logic.

[RESOLUTION] Final condition-changes in dependency order: (1) State-level elimination of single-family-only zoning in metros >100,000 population, incentivized by federal infrastructure funding. (2) Construction visa expansion of 100,000/year to address the 32% labor gap. (3) Tariff exemptions on residential building materials to halt the $10,900/unit cost addition. (4) The Skeptic's split tax regime redirecting investor capital to new-build. (5) Direct federal funding for 500,000 units/year of below-market housing, targeting the 7.1M affordable unit gap. Measurable outcome: close the 4M general supply gap within 7 years; reduce the 7.1M affordable gap to 4M within 10 years; reduce median price-to-income from 5.0 to 3.5 within a decade. Effort-redistribution flag: item (4) redirects private capital rather than changing a material condition, but it is paired with genuine condition changes in items (1)-(3) and (5).

### Skeptic

[CONDITIONS] Short-term rentals number 1.77 million listings in the U.S. as of mid-2025, with 89% being whole-home listings, removing approximately 1.57 million units from long-term housing stock ([Granicus](https://granicus.com/blog/are-short-term-vacation-rentals-contributing-to-the-housing-crisis/)). New York's STR regulations eliminated tens of thousands of listings, yet many units shifted to 30-day minimums rather than returning to long-term occupancy ([Propmodo](https://propmodo.com/short-term-rental-bans-dont-solve-long-term-housing-shortages/)). Dallas's 2025 parking requirement cuts and building code reforms represent the kind of supply-enabling regulatory change that even demand-side advocates should endorse ([Smart Cities Dive](https://www.smartcitiesdive.com/news/zoning-housing-regulation-dallas-trends-2026-outlook/809833/)).

[DEPENDENCY] The Defender's full chain (A through G) is logically sound but has a governance dependency it has not addressed. Federal zoning preemption faces constitutional federalism barriers: land use is a state and local power. The Defender revised to "state-level" reform in Round 3, which I accept, but the dependency should begin with state legislative action, incentivized by federal funding, as 2025 legislative efforts demonstrate ([Mercatus Center](https://www.mercatus.org/research/policy-briefs/housing-reform-states-menu-options-2026)). If step (A) is blocked by jurisdictional constraints, steps (B) through (G) still proceed for labor and cost reform but cannot unlock new land for construction. The chain's first link is politically contingent in a way the Defender has not fully resolved.

[CONTRADICTION] After three rounds, I concede the principal contradiction against my own position: the demand-side framing cannot account for the 7.1 million affordable unit gap identified by NLIHC. No reallocation of existing stock, no investor restriction, and no vacancy tax produces 7.1 million units. Physical construction is necessary. However, supply primacy and market-rate delivery structurally undermine each other for this population: the units needed serve households earning below 30% of area median income, where no market return is possible. Both sides converge on a tension that requires public production, not market resolution. The Defender's trajectory across three rounds (from zoning reform to inclusionary zoning to social housing funding) effectively incorporated demand-side and public-production elements, restructuring the debate from "supply vs. demand" into a question of sequencing and proportion.

[RESOLUTION] Final condition-changes, revised for governance feasibility: (1) State-level zoning reform using federal infrastructure funding as incentive (accepted from Defender with governance correction). (2) Construction workforce expansion via visa reform (accepted from Defender as necessary prerequisite). (3) Split tax regime channeling investor capital from acquisition to production (from my Round 2). (4) STR regulation in markets with rental vacancy <5%, returning an estimated 200,000-400,000 units to long-term stock. (5) Public funding for below-market construction at 150,000 units/year. Measurable outcome: same price-to-income targets as Defender (5.0 to 3.5 in a decade), with governance dependency resolved by starting at the state level. Effort-redistribution flag: item (4) is regulatory redistribution; items (1), (2), and (5) are genuine material condition changes; item (3) is capital redirection that functions as a condition change only if paired with (1) and (2).

## Verdict

### Condition-changes sequenced by dependency order

Each step depends on the prior step being operative:

| Order | Intervention | Type | Depends On | Timeline | Measurable Target |
|-------|-------------|------|------------|----------|-------------------|
| 1 | State-level zoning reform eliminating SFH-only zones in metros >100K, incentivized by federal infrastructure funding | Regulatory condition change | None (prerequisite) | Year 1-3 | Increase by-right multifamily-eligible land by 40% |
| 2 | Construction trade visa expansion (100K/yr) + apprenticeship funding | Material condition change | Partially parallel to (1), but construction depends on (1) for buildable sites | Year 1-3 | Reduce labor shortage from 32% to 15%; reduce build time from 11 to 7 months |
| 3 | Tariff exemptions on residential building materials | Material condition change | Parallel to (1)-(2); enables cost reduction for units produced | Year 1-2 | Eliminate the $10,900/unit tariff surcharge |
| 4 | Split tax regime: higher rates on existing-home investor acquisitions, accelerated depreciation for new-build investment | Capital-direction condition change | Requires (1)-(3) so redirected capital has buildable, staffable, cost-viable projects | Year 2-4 | Shift 20% of investor capital from acquisition to production; 60-80K additional units/yr |
| 5 | STR regulation in tight markets (rental vacancy <5%) | Allocation condition change | Independent but supplementary; recovers existing stock | Year 1-3 | Return 200-400K units to long-term occupancy |
| 6 | Federal social housing funding ($30-40B/yr) for below-market construction | Material condition change | Requires (1) for sites, (2) for labor, (3) for costs | Year 3-10 | 150K-500K permanently affordable units/yr; reduce 7.1M affordable gap to 4M in 10 years |

### Strongest arguments (cited)

**For supply-primacy (Defender):** The physical deficit of 4.03 million units ([Realtor.com](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/housing-supply-gap-surpasses-4-million-homes-in-2025-as-construction-fails-to-keep-pace-with-demand-302701775.html)) and the 7.1 million affordable-unit gap ([NLIHC](https://nlihc.org/news/nlihc-releases-gap-2026-shortage-affordable-homes)) are empirically grounded across independent estimates. Homeowner vacancy at 1.0% ([FRED](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/USHVAC)) is the lowest recorded. The decomposition of investor activity revealed that institutional investors own only 3% of rental stock and are net sellers ([EconFact](https://econofact.org/factbrief/do-private-equity-firms-own-20-of-single-family-homes), [BatchData](https://batchdata.io/blog/real-estate-investor-activity-nationwide)), undermining the financialization narrative at scale. Minneapolis zoning reform produced 16-34% price reductions ([SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5347083)), providing causal evidence that regulatory supply constraints are primary.

**Against supply-primacy (Skeptic):** The 54% home price increase vs. 29% wage increase over five years ([Visual Capitalist](https://www.visualcapitalist.com/why-u-s-homes-feel-pricier-house-prices-vs-income-1985-2025/)) and the 43% vs. 7% gap in 2019-2022 ([Kinder Institute](https://kinder.rice.edu/urbanedge/gap-between-income-growth-and-housing-cost-increases-continues-grow)) demonstrate price acceleration beyond what a 50,000-unit/year shortfall explains. European research showing supply without demand channeling produces "investment objects rather than affordable homes" ([Tandfonline](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/2833115X.2026.2629895)) justifies the split tax regime. The Defender's own evolution across rounds, from pure zoning reform to incorporating social housing funding, conceded that market-rate supply alone is insufficient.

### Structural contradictions (unresolved)

1. **Supply primacy vs. affordability for the bottom 30%.** Market-rate construction at $200-350/sq ft cannot serve households earning below 30% AMI. The primary problem (insufficient units) and the primary market mechanism (private construction) are structurally misaligned for the most affected population. This requires permanent public subsidy with no market resolution. Both sides converged on this point.

2. **Demand suppression vs. construction incentive.** Policies that reduce investor-driven price inflation simultaneously reduce builder profit margins, discouraging the supply response both sides agree is necessary. The split tax regime mitigates but does not eliminate this tension: taxing existing-home acquisitions may reduce overall housing market liquidity, increasing holding periods and reducing turnover that generates move-up supply.

3. **Labor as meta-constraint.** Both public and private construction compete for the same scarce workforce. Scaling social housing (item 6) while expanding market-rate production (items 1-4) requires labor supply to grow faster than total construction demand. If visa expansion or apprenticeship programs underperform, the entire sequenced resolution stalls at step 2.

### Unresolved dependencies (broken links named)

1. **Zoning reform -> actual construction.** Permission to build is necessary but not sufficient. The translation from reformed zoning to housing starts depends on construction costs, labor availability, and developer willingness, all of which are independently constrained. This link is the most fragile in the chain.

2. **Investor capital redirection -> affordable units.** The split tax regime redirects capital toward new-build, but new-build investment targets market-rate or build-to-rent products, not affordable housing. The link from "redirected capital" to "units accessible to median earners" depends on inclusionary requirements or subsidy that the tax regime alone does not guarantee.

3. **STR regulation -> recovered units.** New York's experience shows that STR regulation produced listing removal but not proportionate return to long-term occupancy ([Propmodo](https://propmodo.com/short-term-rental-bans-dont-solve-long-term-housing-shortages/)). The link from "regulation" to "units returned to housing stock" is empirically weak. The 200-400K recovery estimate may overstate actual yield.

4. **Political sequencing.** Steps 1-3 require concurrent action across state legislatures, federal immigration policy, and trade policy. These operate on independent political timelines with no coordinating mechanism. The dependency order is logically sound but assumes a governance capacity that does not currently exist.

### Confidence: 7/10

Supply is the primary problem: confidence 9/10. The physical deficit is measurable, the vacancy data confirms tightness at historically unprecedented levels, and the institutional-investor narrative collapses under decomposition (3% of stock, net sellers). The six-step resolution sequence is logically sound but politically uncertain: confidence 5/10. The demand-side critique does not overturn supply primacy but correctly identifies that supply delivery requires solving capital allocation, labor, and governance problems that pure supply framing ignores. The 7.1M affordable-unit gap is the strongest single datum in the debate, because it cannot be addressed by any demand-side intervention and simultaneously cannot be addressed by market-rate supply. It requires public production, which both sides endorsed but neither fully resolved.
